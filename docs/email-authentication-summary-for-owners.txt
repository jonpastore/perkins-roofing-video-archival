EMAIL SECURITY UPGRADE — PERKINSROOFING.NET
What we did on July 10, 2026, and why it matters

SUMMARY

We upgraded the email security for perkinsroofing.net. In plain terms:

1. Email the company sends is now digitally "signed," so Gmail, Yahoo,
   and Outlook can prove a message really came from Perkins Roofing.

2. We turned on a policy that tells the world's mail providers: "If you
   receive an email claiming to be from perkinsroofing.net and it fails
   that proof, treat it as suspicious and send it to spam."

3. We set up a reporting mailbox so we can see, every day, who is
   sending email using our name — including scammers trying to
   impersonate the company.

The impact: fewer legitimate Perkins emails landing in customers' spam
folders, and real protection against criminals sending fake invoices or
phishing emails that appear to come from us. Roofing companies are a
frequent target for this because of the size of the payments involved.

Nothing changes in day-to-day email use. Nobody needs a new password,
app, or training. This all happens behind the scenes.


THE DETAILS, IN PLAIN ENGLISH

1) THE PROBLEM WE WERE SOLVING

Email was designed decades ago with no built-in way to prove who sent a
message. Anyone can put "perkinsroofing.net" in the FROM line of an
email — the same way anyone can write a false return address on an
envelope. Mail providers deal with this using three published records
that a domain owner sets up. Before today, our setup was incomplete:
our marketing emails were covered, but the everyday email sent by staff
from Gmail was only partially protected. That had two costs:

  - Deliverability: messages from staff, especially when forwarded,
    could fail the checks and land in spam.
  - Impersonation: we had no enforcement telling other mail systems to
    junk emails that fake our name, and no visibility into whether that
    was happening.

2) WHAT WE CHANGED (THE THREE RECORDS)

These are settings published in our domain's DNS — think of DNS as the
public phone book for perkinsroofing.net that every mail provider
checks.

  SPF (already in place, verified): a public list of the mail services
  allowed to send on our behalf — Google (our staff email), our
  marketing/newsletter service, and our automated proposal-reminder
  service. Anything else claiming to be us is not on the list.

  DKIM (new today): the digital signature. We generated a private
  signing key in our Google Workspace and published the matching public
  key in DNS. Every email staff send now carries a tamper-proof
  signature that receiving systems verify automatically. This is the
  piece that was missing — our automated emails were signed, but
  regular staff email was not.

  DMARC (upgraded today): the policy and the reporting. Our old policy
  said "check, but take no action." The new policy says: if an email
  fails both checks above, put it in the recipient's spam folder
  ("quarantine"). It also tells every major mail provider to send us a
  daily summary report of all email seen using our domain name.

3) THE REPORTING GROUP

We created a group mailbox, dmarc@perkinsroofing.net, currently
received by Jon and Amber. The world's mail providers (Google, Yahoo,
Microsoft, and others) will send automated daily reports there. The
reports show how many messages used our name, where they came from,
and whether they passed or failed. This is how we will spot both
delivery problems and impersonation attempts.

One group policy had to be loosened to make this work: the group must
accept email from senders outside the company, because the reports
come from Google, Yahoo, Microsoft, etc. Only this one mailbox accepts
outside mail this way; it cannot be used to reach staff, its member
list is hidden, and only Jon and Amber receive its contents. This was
a deliberate, contained change, not a company-wide one.

4) WHAT TO EXPECT

  - No change for staff. Sending and receiving email works exactly as
    before.
  - Better inbox placement over the coming weeks, as mail providers see
    consistently signed mail from us.
  - Daily technical reports arriving at the dmarc@ mailbox within a day
    or two. They are machine-generated files, not messages needing a
    reply. Down the road we can plug in a free service that turns them
    into a simple weekly summary.
  - If anyone ever reports "I sent an email and it went to spam,"
    that's now a diagnosable problem — the reports will show exactly
    which check failed and why.

5) ONE FUTURE STEP (OPTIONAL)

Today's policy is "quarantine" — fakes go to spam. Once the reports
confirm all our legitimate mail passes cleanly for a few weeks, the
final step is a "reject" policy, where fakes are refused outright and
never reach the recipient at all. We'll recommend that switch when the
data supports it.
